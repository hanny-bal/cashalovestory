What do you think of?

Probably of something illegal. A drug deal maybe. Transactions like this are mostly done by cash. It’s anonymous. And it’s easy to use.

 

Most illegal activities are fuelled by large amounts of cash. Nobody asks where the money comes from when you hand somebody a 50 Euro note. This applies to organised crime but also to illegal labour. In a lot of cities, a rather large black labour market has developed over the years. Activities like that couldn’t happen in a cashless society. At least not in the way it’s happening now. In this regard, also consider prostitution and black markets. All these “industries” are based on physical cash.

Most illegal activities are fuelled by large amounts of cash.

It also works the other way around – when you carry large amounts of cash in your pockets, it’s easy for someone to take it from you with violence. It’s simple. The thief just has to take your wallet. If you only had a bank card with you, the story would look different. You could call the bank as soon as soon as you get home. The thief wouldn’t be able to withdraw any money. He also would have to figure out the pin code in the first place.

 

So it looks like going cashless can provide security and order in a society. In 2016, India wanted to put these findings into reality. The government recalled 86 percent of its cash in order to starve the black markets in the country. In the beginning, the plan seemed to work out. Illegal markets declined and more people began to start a company, bringing large amounts of taxes into the state’s treasury.  Over the course of the years though, all the money the government withdrew flushed back into the black market – making the original recall a bit of a farce.

The Losers of Going Cashless

Not everything works cashless. Especially for the poorer population. Often times, they can’t afford the digital services the would need. Especially if they have a business. Digital payments require infrastructure. And credit card companies usually charge companies a fee for every payment. In a cashless world, people who need the money most get the least of. Credit card companies like Mastercard of Visa on the other hand will most likely get richer.

The people who need the money most get the least of it.

Also, what about old people? A lot of older, retired people rely on cash for everything. Some don’t even own a bank account. The situation might get a bit better in the next few years, when the generation of 40 and 50 year old people retires, but older people still seem to be more sceptical towards digital payments.

 

One extreme and pretty awkward that people still like cash could be seen in Sweden – one of the most progressive countries when it comes to going cashless. In Skonghall, a small town in the northern part of the country, the residents fought for more than half a year to get an ATM machine. They desperately wanted it. A lot of people in this rural area still rely on physical money and the next cash machine was more than 20 Kilometers away. Then they finally got an ATM: And boy, were the people happy. Just to make clear how happy they were – here’s a video of the village’s inhabitants on the day the ATM started operating. They celebrated it with a huge event.

What can we learn out of this? People still demand cash. Even in the most progressive countries.

The Crypto-Alternative

But! There might be an alternative. An alternative to the dependence of banks for the modern society. This alternative is called Bitcoin. Or Ethereum. New, digital, so-called cryptocurrencies based on blockchain. These cryptocurrencies are anonymous and nobody can influence how and what people can pay for.

An alternative called Bitcoin.

The only problem here is: Blockchain currencies aren’t made for mass usage. Bitcoin is as unstable and as unpredictable as a currency can be. This makes it nearly impossible to use as a primary currency. Vendors would have to change prices constantly and not everyone would be able to keep up with the rapid changes in value. But who knows. Maybe in the future, there could be a more reliable, global currency based on blockchain.

No Power

Going cashless also raises the question, what happens when there’s no electricity. When Hurricane Maria hit Puerto Rico, the American Fed had to fly a planeload of cash to the island because the payment infrastructure broke down. There’s no solution regarding technical difficulties like this yet. Time will show if people want to fully rely on technology in this regard. When the financial system is bound to internet and electricity, a country becomes vulnerable. Not only to natural catastrophes like hurricanes but also to hacking attacks. The past has shown – no system is invincible.

What happens when there's no electricity?

Moreover, the whole digital payment infrastructure is provided by banks – institutions most people have no influence on at all on. Relying only on digital payments would make these banks extremely powerful. They could even choose who is allowed to pay for what. For example, banks could forbid customers to pay for guns using their services. While most of you might view this as a good measure, there’s a lot of danger to consider here. Bank and credit card institutions would become more powerful than any other institution in the world. They literally could rule our lives.

Convenience and Carrying Around Money

An interesting case regarding the impact of going cashless is Somaliland. In this African country, one US Dollar is worth 9000 Schilling – while the largest banknote is worth 5000 dollars. So imagine you live in Somaliland and you buy a car. The amount of physical money you had to carry around would be immense. Cashless payments really make people’s lives easier in this case.

Cashless payments make people's lives easier.

When thinking about all the physical money though, paying with a few kilograms of banknotes certainly feels more like “paying” than typing a code on a phone. That’s an aspect to consider. Hot to this extreme extent but to a certain degree. How will the perception of money change? How will our kids view money when they only see a card which people use to pay for everything? There’s no data on this issue yet – but will become a big question in the future for sure.

Conclusion

All in all, going cashless makes people’s lives easier. People enjoy the simplicity and reliability of digital payments. As for now, cash will still be a go-to payment option for the next few decades. But most of the cash around might become more and more obsolete.

 

Will we go fully cashless? Probably not. There are still some obstacles – especially when it comes to certain groups of people. But there will definitely be less cash.

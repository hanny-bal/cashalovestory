  • Should we go cashless?

est. time to read: 3 minutes

Look at your wallet!

How much physical money do you have in your pocket? Probably not too much – at least if you live in Belgium.

 

European countries are getting more and more cashless. Countries like Sweden or Belgium are on their way to ditching cash nearly completely. “Of course”, some of you might think. “Payments with card or phone are much more convenient!”. For most people, they are indeed – and Banks are jumping on the trend and provide innovative phone apps and SIM cards with a NFC function, promoting digital payment methods even more.

Countries are on their way to going nearly full-on cashless.

The reason for the development seems obvious: Digitalisation. All over the planet. And it certainly doesn’t stop at payment methods. The rise of online shops may make a bank account, credit card or a Paypal account mandatory. At least if you didn’t want to experience the drawbacks of being reliant on cash nowadays.

 

Having no physical cash at all still isn’t an option yet though. A lot of small shops like laundry salons or corner cafés still don’t accept digital payments – and they probably also won’t in the future. Digital payments are expensive. Businesses don’t just need machines that accept a card or phone payment but they also need computers and programs to manage all these money transfers. Banks also need servers and IT experts. Furthermore, for every payment made with credit card, the business has to pay a fee to the service provider.

Raising Big Questions

This amount of infrastructure raises one important question: What happens if technology doesn’t work? What if the internet collapses or electricity fails? In a fully cashless society – like in some places in Sweden – the system would probably collapse. At least for some time, there would be no alternative currency to pay with. Of course, the banking sectors have the best security systems they can afford and experts all over the planet. But still, technology can be a weak point here. For more detailed information, check out the section Consequences.

What if the internet collapses or electricity fails?

Cashless payments aren’t just limited to card and phone payments though. In China, for example, two giant tech-companies created their own payment services – Alipay from Alibaba and WeChatPay from the messaging app WeChat. Their payment services are on the rise in China and may eventually get even more popular than traditional bank and credit card payments.

 

In Africa on the other hand, people often use SMS services to pay. Inflation has made some currencies on the content so worthless that people would literally need to carry around kilograms and suitcases of banknotes in order to pay for a loaf of broad. Paying by SMS with their phones makes life easier here.

So What?

Going cashless without a doubt bring some advantages. But it also some severe disadvantages. It makes life easier. Technology is still a risk though. Also, what about collectors who are enthusiastic about physical money? What about old people who aren’t used to digital technology? What about kids? Will they need a bank account at a very young age? These are all questions that need to be answered before taking any serious measures.

 

And of course, there’s a big question about data privacy. Should banks be able to see all of our payments? Get deeper into the topic right in the Consequences section. Or check out research data in the Data section.
